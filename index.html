"The Cloverleaf"; an omnidirectional wind turbine

Orthographic View

Top View

(This is how this design got the name "Cloverleaf")

Demo of the first version's omnidirectional capabilities

3D print of final model

This project was created as part of a bonus challenge in my engineering design projects course. We were tasked with creating a 3D-printed wind turbine that "focused more on form rather than function" and spun around a toothpick axis.

I had the idea to make something helical as I thought those styles of wind turbines had significant visual appeal but I also wanted to add my own twist; to make it spin from wind facing any direction, and be 3D printable without supports.

After consulting a few of my classmates who had previous experience in CAD software and some research, I used Autodesk Inventor to model the part and 3D printed it to test it. The first print was mostly succesful; it met the goal of being omnidirectional but lacked support when printing the top. My first idea was to add curved, drag-type turbine blade in the middle, but it made the turbine too heavy to spin properly. Instead, I made the final model with a thin, lightweight tube in the middle: this is where the toothpick would sit and also acted as a support during the print.

Overall, this project was a lot of fun. I was ecstatic to see that the turbine was omnidirectional on the first try. It also improved my skill with Autodesk Inventor and my design thinking for manufactuing.

2021 MEC Junior Design Challenge 

Orthographic view of the vehicle

Top view of the vehicle

Submission video demonstrating the vehicle's abilities

This project came to life during the 2021 McMaster Engineering Challenge. Being only a first-year, my team and I joined the junior design challenge, with the goal of creating a fleet of vehicles that completed four total challenges; to travel more than 3m, carry an object, knock an object over, and be made of less than 5 materials. 

Once the competition started, we took some inspiration from online, particulary this video by Mark Rober on how tocreate our drivetrain. Then, together with two of my teammates, Anshul Keswani and Yuxi Qin, we created what we lovingly dubbed "The 3-Piece McNugget Meal"; a cardboard-based vehicle that tackled three of the challenges at once, being able to carry about the weight of an iPhone, travelling 5 metres and being capable of knocking over a water bottle. We were able to create this vehicle and a pitch presentation including a video in under 6 hours. The time limit was easily our biggest challenge in completing the project. 

Our vehicle made it to finals and we recieved great feedback on our pitch and design. However, we were unfortunately disqualified as the challenege required four separate vehicles for each challenge rather than an all-in-one design; something we misunderstood when communicating with the event coordinators. That weekend, my team and I learned a lot about double-checking project requirements and being more careful in planning out and managing our time, but we also improved our communication and had a blast anyways.

1P13 Cornerstone Design Projects

Turbine blade deflection simulation done in Autodesk Inventor

Virtual robotic arm environment where the Python program was executed

As part of McMaster's revamped engineering program, each student, over the course of their first year works in a team of 4-5 other students to complete four different projects. Each time, using a diverse skill set useful to all engineering streams, including CAD, coding, design communication and teamwork.

Each semester will be focused on one project, and so far I've completed two: 

The first was to do a materials selection for a wind turbine based on a certain scenario, ranging from  powering LEDs in a Guatemalan city to designing a large-scale turbine to power several Swedish cities.

The second project was split in two; one team would create a Python 3 program to use a robotic arm to transport containers to an autoclave for remote sterilization, and the other team would use CAD to design a container, to hold a specific tool and to fit in a specific footprint.

You can find more details about these projects on my dedicated Notion site.

McMaster Baja Racing | Nov. 2021 - Present

McMaster Baja Racing is McMaster's most accomplished competitive engineering team. Each year, we build a new, sinlge-seat offroad vehicle to compete in the SAE Baja Series against international teams from across North America. 

As part of the chassis subteam, we are responsible for designing and manufacturing the pipe frame and cowl components of the vehicle. So far, I've contributed to the front and rear cowls the most, in modifying the CAD files of the front cowl mould to minimize failures, collaborating with other members of my subteam to assemble the mould, and completing research to optimize the procedure for assembly using carbon fibre. I also reattached the rear cowl to improve its structural integrity and aesthetics.

The McMaster Engineering I Discord Server one of the biggest first-year discord servers with 1300+ members. As one of 8 volunteer administrators on the server, I'm responsible for ensuring discussion in the server stays relevant and the community stays as open and welcoming to all students. Admins are also responsible for ensuring the server stays clear of any phishing, and to lockdown the server before any midterms or exams to ensure the academic honesty of all of the students.

I've also hosted 4 different help sessions before midterms and exams, for general chemistry and physics mechanics. They've been a resounding success upon hearing positive feedback from my classmates, having 200+ total attend live and 700+ total views of the recordings on YouTube. I've also been active asynchronously, anwering student questions and offering hints and step-by-step full solutions where needed, entirely free of cost to help mitigate the challenges students face with online learning and the ongoing pandemic.
